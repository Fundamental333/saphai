About SAPHAI

SAPHAI proposes a dedicated forum to examine how security, agency, and privacy jointly shape conversational and agentic systems.

The rapid emergence of conversational AI, generative systems, and autonomous agents is reshaping how data is collected and operationalized in human-AI ecosystems. These systems process sensitive personal data, integrate multimodal signals, construct behavioral profiles, and increasingly act on behalf of users. In doing so, they mediate agency, decision-making, and identity formation.

Within these environments, security, agency, and privacy are deeply interdependent. Security failures can undermine autonomy, privacy violations can expose identity, and constrained agency can amplify systemic risks. Yet these dimensions are often addressed separately or only after deployment.

SAPHAI brings together researchers from HCI, security, privacy, AI systems, and social computing to examine these dimensions as interconnected foundations of human-AI interaction. The workshop explores privacy risks, identity inference, multimodal leakage, delegated authority, governance challenges, and privacy-centered prototypes for agentic systems.

Motivation

Conversational AI systems, generative models, and autonomous agents are increasingly embedded in everyday communication, decision making, governance, and knowledge work. Unlike earlier interactive systems that responded to discrete user commands, emerging agentic architectures infer user intentions, retrieve and synthesize information across modalities, coordinate actions across external services, and execute tasks on behalf of users.

These systems maintain persistent memory, construct evolving user profiles, and generate representations of identity that accumulate over time. They therefore participate not only in information exchange, but in shaping how individuals are represented, interpreted, and acted upon within sociotechnical environments.

Agentic systems also alter the conditions under which agency is exercised. When users delegate tasks to conversational agents, they rely on the system to interpret intent, act faithfully, and respect contextual boundaries. Delegation in this setting is not merely functional. It is epistemic and representational: the system decides what to retrieve, what to prioritize, what actions to execute, and how the user is characterized in downstream interactions.

Privacy in conversational systems therefore extends beyond traditional concerns of storage and access control. Agentic systems infer latent traits, predict preferences, detect vulnerabilities, and generate outputs that may expose sensitive attributes about individuals or groups. Multimodal integration of text, voice, images, behavioral telemetry, and contextual metadata increases the likelihood of cross-modal leakage, identity inference, adversarial manipulation, and unintended disclosure.

Existing approaches to AI security and privacy have advanced adversarial robustness, model safety, differential privacy, red teaming, and compliance. Still, these approaches often treat privacy and security as post-hoc constraints. In agentic architectures, privacy and security are not peripheral safeguards. They structure how agency is enacted.

SAPHAI is motivated by three core objectives:

  • Systematically examine emerging privacy and security risks in human-AI ecosystems, including multimodal leakage, persistent identity construction, delegated authority abuse, and cross-platform data integration.
  • Investigate architectural mechanisms that embed privacy and security into agentic systems in ways that preserve accountable and meaningful human agency.
  • Articulate a forward-looking research agenda that treats security, agency, and privacy as interdependent properties of conversational AI systems rather than isolated concerns.
In alignment with the broader CUI 2026 focus on agency and identities, SAPHAI examines how conversational systems construct, mediate, and constrain identities, and how these processes can be designed to support equitable, transparent, and trustworthy forms of delegation.

How We Will Solicit Papers and Attendees

Call for Participation

We will publish a detailed Call for Participation that clearly articulates the privacy-centered vision of SAPHAI and its focus on agency, identity, and security in conversational and agentic systems. The call will position the workshop as a venue for advancing rigorous, interdisciplinary scholarship that treats privacy and security not as peripheral safeguards but as foundational conditions shaping delegated autonomy, identity construction, and governance in human-AI ecosystems.

The workshop will invite short position papers, prototypes, design provocations, and early-stage research artifacts. We welcome conceptual, empirical, technical, policy-oriented, and design-based contributions.

Targeted Outreach

To ensure broad and interdisciplinary participation, the Call for Participation will be circulated through academic mailing lists, professional networks, AI and privacy communities, interdisciplinary technology-policy forums, university and lab newsletters, and public platforms such as LinkedIn, Mastodon, and related research channels.

We will also conduct targeted outreach to scholars and practitioners whose work bridges conversational systems and privacy, including researchers working on memory systems, agent delegation, multimodal inference, contextual integrity, automation bias, identity construction, and governance of AI systems.

Interdisciplinary Research Community Engagement

SAPHAI focuses on privacy, security, and agency in conversational and agentic systems. Addressing these challenges requires engagement across multiple research communities. No single discipline fully captures the technical, design, and governance issues that arise when AI systems infer identities, manage sensitive data, and act on behalf of users.

  • HCI researchers studying identity, agency, and human-AI interaction
  • Privacy and usable security scholars examining data protection, behavioral inference, and user-facing security mechanisms
  • AI systems and multimodal model researchers developing conversational and agentic architectures
  • Policy and governance scholars studying regulation, accountability, and institutional oversight of AI systems
  • Industry practitioners building conversational agents, AI copilots, and autonomous systems in real-world settings

Website and Communication

SAPHAI will maintain a dedicated workshop website as the central hub for information dissemination, community engagement, and long-term visibility. The website will be managed by the Website Chair, Siddharth Das Sarkar, and will include the workshop description, goals, important dates, submission guidance, review criteria, accessibility information, organizer biographies, keynote updates, and post-workshop materials where appropriate.

The site is intended to remain active beyond the event to archive outcomes, support continuity, and help position SAPHAI as a recurring workshop and future symposium.

Technical Requirements

SAPHAI is designed as an interactive, discussion-oriented workshop and requires only minimal infrastructure.

  • Projection and display: projector and screen for keynote presentations, paper sessions, and prototype demonstrations.
  • Wi-Fi connectivity: reliable internet for demonstrations, shared documents, and collaborative work.
  • Power access: accessible power outlets for presenters and participants using laptops and mobile devices.
  • Whiteboard or writable surface: support for collaborative brainstorming and synthesis during breakout sessions.
  • Participant devices: attendees are encouraged to bring laptops or tablets for collaborative note-taking and shared digital exercises.

No specialized hardware, mandatory software installation, or programming prerequisite is required.

How We Will Create an Accessible and Inclusive Experience

Accessible Materials and Communication

All workshop materials, including the Call for Participation, website content, and session guidelines, will follow accessibility best practices. Materials will use clear language, readable formatting, accessible PDFs, high-contrast visual design, and alt text for images where applicable. Where feasible, materials will be shared in advance to support varied preparation needs.

Inclusive Participation Formats

The workshop will incorporate multiple modes of participation, including short structured presentations, moderated discussion, small-group breakout sessions, and opportunities for written contribution during collaborative exercises. If hybrid participation is possible, remote attendees will be supported through shared documents and active facilitation.

Diverse Representation and Outreach

We will actively solicit participation across geographic regions, institution types, and career stages. Particular attention will be given to early-career and doctoral researchers, interdisciplinary scholars, and researchers from underrepresented and historically marginalized communities.

Code of Conduct and Respectful Dialogue

The workshop will adhere to ACM and SIGCHI codes of conduct. Organizers will establish shared norms for respectful discussion and actively moderate conversations to ensure equitable participation and to prevent dominance by any single perspective.

Sustained Inclusion Beyond the Workshop

To support continuity and broader access, we intend to share a post-workshop summary or collaborative report, provide channels for continued engagement, and encourage follow-up collaboration across institutions and disciplines.
